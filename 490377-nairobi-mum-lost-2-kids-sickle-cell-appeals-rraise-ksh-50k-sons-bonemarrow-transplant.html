Nairobi Mum Who Lost 2 Kids to Sickle Cell Seeks Help to Raise KSh 50k for Son's Bone Marrow Transpl

Publish date: 2024-06-02
  • Beatrice Odera was working as a nurse when her firstborn son was diagnosed with sickle cell anaemia at the age of 0ne year and two months
  • They started him on management treatment and four years later, she welcomed her second-born son who was also diagnosed with sickle cell anaemia
  • All her children were diagnosed with the same disease except for her last born whom she did not want to test and years later, she lost her first two sons
  • Two of his boys require a bone marrow transplant to be done in the US but the family's coffers have run dry yet they are required to raise KSh 32 million

When Beatrice Anyango Odero welcomed her firstborn child, she was hopeful of a happy fulfilling future with a beautiful family, however, her joy was shortlived after a disease visited her family.

Five kids with sickle cell

Odero, a trained nurse, is a mum of five boys but she lost her first two sons to sickle cell anaemia which was also diagnosed in her other three sons.

Speaking to tUKO.co.ke's Linda Shiundu, Odero said her firstborn son was diagnosed with sickle anaemia when he was one year and two months old after exhibiting sickle cell-like symptoms.

"When the results came, It was a very painful experience. Even my colleagues saw the results before me, everybody took off. Nobody wanted to results to me," she narrated."When I saw it, it is like I died on the road. I only read it in books. I had never seen a single child or even an adult suffering from Sickle cell," she added.

She immediately started treatment for the boy and when he was four years old, Odero welcomed a healthy second-born son but having known the condition of their first child, they were advised by their paediatrician to do a sickling test which came back positive.

They started the journey with sick children and even welcomed her third-born son who was also tested for sickle cell and to Odero's disappointment, the results were positive.

Lost first son

When she welcomed her fourth, she was also disappointed after he also tested positive for sickle cell anaemia and when she welcomed her fifth child, she was so heartbroken to even test him for sickle cell.

"Each time a child is tested, in your head you are like, 'so now this is anther challenge to deal with as a mother. Will it be possible fore me?" said Odero."My friends all manner of things. Some who are medics. They told me you can do this, you can try that, but because of my belief, i could not do some of those things,"she added.

Odero braced herself and had hoped her children would live and even put her best foot forward making sure they all have the needed medicine and giving them the best she can afford.

Despite her efforts, Odero lost her second-born son in October 2000 after he developed a fever and started twitching at that point, she lost hope and started asking God questions.

" I went into depression after burial and stayed in the hospital for three months. Later. on I picked up my pieces to continue caring for her other sons," she said.

Bone marrow treatment

Odero once again lost her firstborn son when he was supposed to start his second year at the university, in October 2016 months after he developed a problem with his toe.

Sickle cell disease has not only robbed Odero of her two sons but her first marriage too. She, however, has a loving and supportive husband now.

"When I lost Ron, my life went into a ditch. He was 20 years old. He had learnt a lot of things. He had really mentored his brothers. He was an icon to his brothers and gave them a reason to smile every day," she said.

Currently, the family has been battling her third-born son's bad health in 2019 while he was about to do his Kenya Certificate of Primary Education (KCPE) but he managed to perform well and scored 322 marks.

However, after he joined High School at Jamhuri Boys, he has been in and out of the hospital and at that point, the doctors advised her to seek a bone marrow transplant and that is what they are seeking to get at John Hopkins Hospital in the US.

The procedure requires the family to pay KSh 32 million, which the family can not raise on their own and are appealing to the public to help them raise the funds to get their son the much-needed treatment.
