Here's a brief history of the newspaper print

Publish date: 2024-04-12

If, after two years of its reign, you have tired of leopard print, then you might be pleased to hear that another vintage motif is taking over as fashion’s favourite print – newspaper.

First made famous by Elsa Schiaparelli in the 1930s, but later storming into mainstream culture thanks to John Galliano and Carrie Bradshaw, the print has seen a renaissance on the catwalk over the past few seasons, and now seems to be on track to be the next ‘It’ print.

Whether Kendall Jenner can convince you to try the teeny newsy bikini or you want to wrap yourself in Diane von Furstenberg’s latest design, chances are you are going to be seeing newspaper headlines everywhere over the next season.

As it returns once again, we break down the fascinating history of the print.

Newspaper print first entered the fashion stratosphere back in 1935 when couturier Elsa Schiaparelli took press clippings written about her and transformed them into printed blouses and accessories. The designer noted in her autobiography that this idea came about after being inspired when travelling to Denmark on holiday, where she saw fishwives in Copenhagen wearing hats made of newspaper.

But, as fashion’s favourite watchdog Diet Prada notes, Schiaparelli was not actually the first to find style inspiration in the written newspaper print. The Instagram account cleared up the common misconception this week by sharing an image of a newspaper-printed gown that was worn by Matilda Butters, the wife of an Australian politician, back in 1886.

Over the century since this first occurred, the print has experienced its fair share of controversial moments which are etched into fashion history.

The first of these came from Louis Réard, the inventor of the bikini, who made headlines across the globe when he unveiled the two-piece, which was covered in a newspaper print. If intentional, using a print suggesting something newsworthy was certainly a clever move for what would become one of the most provocative fashion moments of all time.

The next major newspaper-print moment – and likely its most famous – came courtesy of John Galliano’s spring/summer 2000 couture collection for Dior. The designer, no stranger to courting controversy, made headlines with his headline-printed pieces, which were inspired by homeless people sleeping on newspapers on the streets of Paris and a nod to the 1920s Tramp Balls, where the rich would dress up as the poor for fun. Criticised by many for appropriating poverty, the collection has become one of his most well known, also, in part, thanks to one very famous fictional TV character.

One of the Noughties’ biggest fashion influencers, Sex and the City’s Carrie Bradshaw wore one of the newspaper-printed Dior dresses for a scene where she went for lunch with Big’s wife Natasha, following the couple’s affair. The style moment became so iconic that it was revisited for the second movie and we saw Sarah Jessica Parker don the famous frock one more time.

New Line Cinema/Hbo/Village Roadshow/Kobal/Shutterstock//Rex

Now today, almost 20 years after Galliano's collection, newspaper print is back in favour with fashion as the likes of Balenciaga, Calvin Klein, Versace, Helmut Lang and Diane von Furstenberg all find their own spin on the iconic print.

For example, spring/summer 2018 saw Demna Gvasalia back the return of newspaper print in a big way for Balenciaga, covering demure cardigans and crisp shirts in the print as a reflection of “fake news”, the Collins word of the year in 2017 - meaning misinformation in the media - made famous by a tweeting Donald Trump. The brand’s follow-up campaign also nodded to this, seeing Stella Tennant in the newspaper-print designs shielding herself from the glare of paparazzi photographers.

And, most recently, for the upcoming autumn season, Diane von Furstenberg directly mimicked what fellow designer Schiaparelli had done decades before, taking newspaper articles written about her and turning them into a print. For DVF, this fittingly covered a wrap dress, the most iconic and newsworthy design in the fashion house's history.

So, as more designers jump back on board the newspaper-print trend, and celebrities too – Kim Kardashian West wore a vintage Dior skirt recently, while her sister Kendall Jenner donned a teeny newspaper-print bikini and supermodel Elsa Hosk embraced headlines all over her trousers – it seems that the newspaper print may be back in fashion’s favour and set to rule the next few seasons, 133 years and many controversial moments since it first emerged.
